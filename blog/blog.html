"Honey, I Lit the House on Fire!"

A Commentary on Bumbling Dads

Commercials are unavoidable cultural elements that shape the way our society thinks about a variety of topics and objects, both blatantly and subtly. Fathers, and the character archetype of a “bumbling dad” who can only do so much as keep his children alive, have been expressly molded by commercials, and subsequently normalized incompetence in a father figure where it shouldn’t be. Society seems to even give off the vibe that having the ability to clean house, cook food, take care of children, or even using a maximum of six muscles to wipe a counter down with Lysol disinfecting wipes is somehow grounds to be looked down upon by other men who don’t have those skills-- it’s hilariously counterintuitive, since, generally, having more skills makes one look better. The commercials in question have wives treating their husbands like their own children--
“Honey, I think you need more fiber in your diet,”
“But Carol, fiber makes me...sad.”
--looking after them and essentially being the keeper and guardian of the “No Fun Zone,” when in reality most relationships between people are egalitarian and have equal effort from both parties. Do these commercials and archetypes influence the way young people think? Did they influence how parents and those who have authority think? Does this influence travel as far to policymakers and those with power? And if the answer to all of these questions is a resounding “yes,” then does the media hold more power over the population than initially thought?
This essay will be an object lesson delving deep into why we as an American culture think of men as helpless homemakers who somehow accidentally light their entire kitchen on fire. It will also look at the way other countries’ media present parents--especially fathers-- and compare and contrast their presentation to our presentation of parents in the United States. Why is the “bumbling father” trope harmful, both to men and women? Is the era of this trope over, or are we just hopeful about moving on from the rampant subtle sexism in our society? Is being a competent parent emasculating, or is the femininity associated with housework as negative as the media makes society think (or both?) These questions will be explored in depth in this object lesson to explain why American society thinks the way it does about parenthood by men, women, and everyone in between, and how it affects the culture and politics for parents and children alike.

Visualizing Sound

A Look at Frequencies of Sound through Frequencies of Light

Ever wondered what sound would look like if you paired it to a frequency of light? We did. Using the MSGEQ7 chip we split frequencies of sound from our input into 7 different bands and assigned them to frequency of light which would be displayed on our RGB LED's. The result is pretty slick. (None of the music used in the video is ours and is used for presentation purposes only.)

  • Date: May 2016
  • For: Senior Capstone Experience
  • Category: Hardware/Arduino

Ball'r

An early disease detection system for the male reproductive system.

Male reproductive health is often overlooked in society. We decided to tackle this problem by making it easier to manage through a small clip-on device and a partner application that will monitor the temperature of the user's testicles and alert the user to any temperature anomalies. This device could act as an early detection system for not only testicular cancer, but also for extranodal non-Hodgkins lymphoma and infirtility issues.
